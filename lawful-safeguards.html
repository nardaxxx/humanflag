Lawful Operational Safeguards in AI Systems

Surrender Recognition, Compliance Architecture, and International Humanitarian Law

Giovanni Nardacci — Founder, Human Flag Association
humanflag.org
Working Paper — May 2026 (Third Revised Edition, July 2026)

Abstract

Contemporary discussions concerning AI safeguards frequently frame such mechanisms as discretionary policy choices, corporate alignment preferences, or operational limitations imposed for safety or reputational reasons. This paper proposes a narrower and more legally grounded distinction: certain categories of safeguards constitute operational mechanisms relevant to compliance with already-existing obligations under International Humanitarian Law (“IHL”).

International Humanitarian Law does not merely protect persons hors de combat; it imposes duties of recognition and verification in terms. Article 41(1) of Additional Protocol I protects a person “who is recognized or who, in the circumstances, should be recognized” to be hors de combat; Article 57(2)(a)(i) requires those who plan or decide upon an attack to do everything feasible to verify that the objectives to be attacked are not protected from attack and are military objectives; and Article 36 obliges States party to the Protocol to determine, at the stage of study, development, acquisition or adoption of any new weapon, means or method of warfare, whether its employment would be prohibited by existing law. For States not party to Additional Protocol I, a comparable review inquiry may arise, or be operationally expected, through customary precautionary duties, national weapons-review policies, or institutional procurement standards — on a separate procedural basis, addressed in Section III.

This paper argues that, read together, these provisions make surrender-recognition capability — the technical capacity of a system to identify, preserve, or escalate human surrender and hors de combat indicators — a necessary question for Article 36 review where that provision applies, and for comparable weapons-review or procurement processes where the same compliance risks arise on a separate legal or policy basis. Not a new obligation, but the effectiveness condition of obligations that have bound States for decades.

A central premise of the analysis is temporal: as operational decision cycles contract below the threshold of meaningful human assessment, human supervision risks becoming nominal rather than substantive. Under such conditions, an embedded surrender-recognition safeguard may be one of the few mechanisms capable of operating within the window in which lethal action is determined.

The paper does not argue that international law mandates any particular AI architecture, nor that autonomous or semi-autonomous systems are unlawful as a class. It advances a determinate proposition: the systematic exclusion of technically achievable safeguards relevant to surrender recognition and de-escalation engages existing duties of recognition, verification, and legal review — and therefore warrants documented justification rather than silence.

Revision note: This third revised edition clarifies the treaty/customary-law distinction concerning Article 36, refines the role of Article 57 as procedural support for the Article 41 recognition duty, narrows and defines the scope of ‘military-adjacent’ systems, reformulates technical maturity criteria as documented reference points rather than fixed thresholds, specifies the ordinary elements conditioning any command-responsibility analysis, and updates institutional sources. The central thesis is unchanged.
